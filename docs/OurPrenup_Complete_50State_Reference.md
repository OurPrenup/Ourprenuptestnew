**Alabama** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]* **⚠ CONDITIONAL ATTORNEY REQ**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Disputed. Has own statute at Ala. Code § 30-4-9;
framework aligns with UPAA but adoption status is contested across
sources.

**Statute:** Ala. Code § 30-4-9 (2007)

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by party giving up rights
(best practice: both sign). No consideration needed.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. General picture of finances
sufficient; extremely detailed breakdowns not required. Certified
financial statements recommended.

**Independent Counsel:** CONDITIONALLY REQUIRED. Two-prong test (Nelson
v. Nelson 2010; Hollar v. Hollar 2023): Enforcing party must prove
EITHER (a) other spouse viewed agreement as fair/just/equitable, OR (b)
other spouse entered freely with competent independent advice and full
knowledge. If agreement is NOT inherently fair, attorneys become a HARD
requirement.

**Notarization:** Not required by statute. Strongly recommended.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. Courts have upheld
prenups signed day before wedding IF opportunity to consult attorney
existed.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated. Public assistance
safety valve: court can override if one party would become eligible for
public assistance.

**Unconscionability:** At time of execution.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** Unique fairness-OR-process enforcement test. If fair
on face, enforced without attorneys. If not fair, attorneys required.
Fraud (hiding assets) invalidates. Amendments must be written and signed
by both.

**Key Case Law:** Nelson v. Nelson (2010), Hollar v. Hollar (2023),
Ayers v. Ayers (2022), Williams v. Williams (2014).

**Platform Notes:** MODERATE RISK for self-service. Must either ensure
substantive fairness OR require attorney review. Include waiver of
counsel option with risk disclosure.

**Alaska** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]* **⚠ OPT-IN COMMUNITY PROPERTY**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution (with opt-in community
property under AS 34.75)

**UPAA/UPMAA:** NOT adopted. Governed by case law and general contract
principles.

**Statute:** No specific prenup statute. Community property: AS 34.75.
Divorce: AS 25.24.160.

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both parties.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. Full and fair. Community property
agreements specifically require full disclosure OR voluntary written
waiver.

**Independent Counsel:** Recommended, not required.

**Notarization:** Recommended, not required.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period.

**Spousal Support:** Can be addressed. Courts ensure maintenance fairly
allocates economic effect of dissolution.

**Unconscionability:** Must be \'legally procured and ostensibly fair.\'

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** UNIQUE OPT-IN COMMUNITY PROPERTY. Under AS 34.75,
couples can execute a community property agreement or trust to opt into
community property for some/all assets. Can be done before or during
marriage (not after divorce filing). Platform should ask whether couple
wants to opt in.

**Key Case Law:** Batey v. Batey, 933 P.2d 551 (1997). Limited
AK-specific prenup case law.

**Platform Notes:** Unusual state. Platform needs special handling for
opt-in community property feature. Otherwise standard requirements make
it self-service friendly.

**Arizona** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]*

**Property Regime:** Community Property

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes. ARS §§ 25-201 to 25-205.

**Statute:** ARS §§ 25-201 to 25-205

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both parties. No
consideration needed. Effective upon marriage.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. Fair and reasonable. Can be waived
in writing, but lack of disclosure combined with unconscionability
invalidates.

**Independent Counsel:** Recommended, not required. Presence of counsel
strengthens enforceability.

**Notarization:** Not required by statute. Recommended as extra
protection.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated. Public assistance
safety valve applies. Courts can refuse to enforce alimony waivers under
certain conditions.

**Unconscionability:** At time of execution.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** Community property state --- without prenup, most
property acquired during marriage split 50/50. Standard UPAA with no
significant deviations. Lifestyle clauses (infidelity penalties, chore
requirements) not enforceable. Clauses encouraging divorce may be struck
down. Postnuptial agreements available but cannot be obtained after
marriage as a \'prenup.\'

**Key Case Law:** Standard UPAA application. No landmark unique cases
identified.

**Platform Notes:** Good self-service candidate. Community property
clauses needed (transmutation, income classification, commingling).

**Arkansas** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]* **⚠ ACKNOWLEDGMENT REQUIRED (4
OPTIONS)**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes. Adopted 1987. A.C.A. §§ 9-11-401 to 9-11-413.

**Statute:** A.C.A. §§ 9-11-401 to 9-11-413

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed AND ACKNOWLEDGED by both
parties. No consideration needed.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. Standard UPAA. Can be waived in
writing.

**Independent Counsel:** Recommended, not required. One of the 4
acknowledgment options involves attorney affirmation.

**Notarization:** One of 4 acknowledgment options (see below).

**Witnesses:** One of 4 acknowledgment options (see below).

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period.

**Spousal Support:** Can be addressed. Public assistance safety valve
applies (§ 9-11-406(b)).

**Unconscionability:** At time of execution.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** ACKNOWLEDGMENT REQUIREMENT (§ 9-11-402): Must be
satisfied by ONE of four methods: (1) Formal declaration before an
authorized public officer. (2) Sworn affirmation by respective attorneys
that each party understands and consents. (3) Notary-witnessed signing
with statements that parties consulted attorneys, read/understand
agreement, entered freely. (4) Execution witnessed by 2 disinterested
parties. IMPORTANT: Previous research incorrectly stated
\'notarization + 2 witnesses required.\' In reality, you only need ONE
of these four methods. Option 4 (2 witnesses) is the simplest for
self-service. Also: Arkansas courts hold prenups must be made in
contemplation of lasting marriage, not solely anticipating divorce.

**Key Case Law:** Franks v. Franks, 548 S.W.3d 871 (2018). Banks v.
Banks (termination requires written agreement).

**Platform Notes:** Self-service viable. Use acknowledgment option 4 (2
disinterested witnesses) as the default path. Platform must clearly
instruct on signing ceremony. Alternatively, option 3 (notary + specific
statements) works for online notarization.

**California** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]* **⚠ 7-DAY RULE \| ATTORNEY REQ
FOR SPOUSAL SUPPORT \| WRITTEN WAIVER OF COUNSEL \| NOTARIZATION REQ**

**Property Regime:** Community Property

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes with major modifications. CA Family Code §§
1600-1617.

**Statute:** CA Family Code §§ 1600-1617. Key: § 1612 (spousal support),
§ 1615 (enforcement/7-day rule).

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both. Notarized. Effective
upon marriage.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. \'Adequate knowledge\' standard.
Each party must receive complete info about other\'s properties, debts,
income.

**Independent Counsel:** REQUIRED if prenup modifies/eliminates spousal
support (§ 1612(c)). If party has NO attorney: must be advised IN
WRITING of rights being relinquished, AND execute a SEPARATE written
waiver of counsel. Per Marriage of Clarke & Akel (2018), even the
drafting party needs these protections if unrepresented.

**Notarization:** REQUIRED.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** 7-DAY MANDATORY WAITING PERIOD (§ 1615(c)(2)(B)).
Both parties must have 7 calendar days between presentation of FINAL
agreement and signing. Applies to ALL prenups after Jan 1, 2020,
regardless of attorney status. Substantive changes restart the clock.
Cannot be waived. DIFFERENT FROM MN: CA = 7 days from final draft to
signing. MN = 7 days before wedding.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated ONLY if both parties
have independent counsel (§ 1612(c)). Without attorneys, spousal support
provisions unenforceable. Courts have broad discretion to reject
unconscionable waivers. Court can order temporary support while
litigating prenup validity. TIP: Step-up clauses or conditional limits
increase enforceability.

**Unconscionability:** Primarily at execution, but courts can also
consider whether enforcement would be unconscionable (especially spousal
support).

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** FIDUCIARY DUTY: Spouses must act as fiduciaries re:
community property; prenup should include acknowledgment. COMMUNITY
PROPERTY: Without prenup, most property acquired during marriage owned
equally. 7-day rule does NOT apply to postnups. SUNSET CLAUSES allowed.
START 3-6 MONTHS before wedding.

**Key Case Law:** Marriage of Clarke & Akel (2018): Written waiver
required for unrepresented parties. Marriage of Cadwell-Faso & Faso
(2011): 7-day rule for unrepresented (pre-2020). Marriage of Bonds
(2000): Voluntariness scrutiny.

**Platform Notes:** MOST COMPLEX STATE. Must: (1) Enforce 7-day timer in
workflow, (2) Detect spousal support modification → require attorney,
(3) Generate separate written waiver of counsel, (4) Include fiduciary
duty acknowledgment, (5) Require notarization, (6) Community property
clauses.

**Platform Notes:** Self-service possible. MUST generate plain-language

**Connecticut** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]* **⚠ DUAL-TIME UNCONSCIONABILITY
\| OPPORTUNITY FOR COUNSEL REQ**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes --- modified UPAA. CT Premarital Agreement Act (P.A.
95-170, effective Oct 1, 1995). Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 46b-36a to 46b-36j.

**Statute:** Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 46b-36a to 46b-36j

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. \'Fair and reasonable disclosure\'
of property, obligations, income. Need not be exact but must give
general approximation. Insufficient income info has invalidated prenups.

**Independent Counsel:** OPPORTUNITY REQUIRED (§ 46b-36g(a)(4)). Each
party must have reasonable opportunity to consult independent counsel.
If denied, agreement unenforceable.

**Notarization:** Recommended, not required.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. But presenting 1 week
before wedding alone isn\'t disqualifying if concept discussed months
earlier.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated. Court can override if
one party would become eligible for public assistance (§ 46b-36g(b)).

**Unconscionability:** DUAL-TIME REVIEW (§ 46b-36g(a)(2)). Unenforceable
if unconscionable at execution OR when enforcement is sought. CT added
\'at enforcement\' to standard UPAA. Agreement fair when signed can be
invalidated years later if circumstances changed dramatically. McHugh
three-prong test: (1) validly entered, (2) no statute/policy violation,
(3) circumstances at dissolution so beyond contemplation as to cause
injustice. Standard is HIGH --- must \'surprise or shock the
conscience.\'

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** Dual-time review is the critical differentiator.
Arbitration clauses in prenups upheld. Bad bargain not enough; must be
outrageous.

**Key Case Law:** McHugh v. McHugh (1980): Three-prong test. Grabe v.
Hokin (2019): Elaborated on enforcement unconscionability. Bedrick v.
Bedrick (2011): Car accident preventing work made alimony waiver
unconscionable. McKenna v. Delente (2010): Upheld despite disparity.
Dornemann v. Dornemann (2004): Must shock conscience. Friezo v. Friezo
(2007): Arbitration upheld.

**Platform Notes:** Self-service possible but include STRONG fairness
warnings. Recommend periodic review/amendment. Document opportunity for
counsel.

**Delaware** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]*

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes. Adopted 1996. Del. Code Ann. tit. 13, §§ 321-328.

**Statute:** Del. Code Ann. tit. 13, §§ 321-328

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both. No consideration
needed.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. Fair and reasonable. Silverman v.
Silverman: minor omissions (vehicle, \$3K life insurance, misstated
ownership interest) did not invalidate --- substantial compliance
sufficient.

**Independent Counsel:** Recommended, not required.

**Notarization:** Recommended, not required. Strengthens enforceability.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. But signing shortly
before ceremony raises voluntariness concerns.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated. NOTE: Delaware REMOVED
the public assistance safety valve from the UPAA (§ 6(b) language
removed). Courts may NOT be able to override spousal support waivers
even if one party ends up on welfare. Unique among UPAA states.

**Unconscionability:** At time of execution. Statute of limitations
tolled during marriage; laches defense available.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** Removed UPAA public assistance override for spousal
support --- makes Delaware more permissive on support waivers. Prenups
can be set up for finite time periods (sunset clauses). Laches doctrine
can bar late challenges (Sullivan case: 30-year delay barred).

**Key Case Law:** Silverman v. Silverman: Minor disclosure omissions OK.
Sullivan estate case: Laches barred challenge after 30+ years.

**Platform Notes:** Good self-service candidate. No unusual formality
requirements. Note the removed public assistance safety valve when
drafting spousal support provisions.

**Florida** *\[DEEPLY RESEARCHED\]* **⚠ 2 WITNESSES + NOTARY FOR REAL
ESTATE/ESTATE PROVISIONS**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes. Adopted 2007 with modifications. Fla. Stat. §
61.079.

**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 61.079. Also: § 732.702 (estate waivers).

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both. No consideration
needed.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. Full and frank. Florida is \'in
between\' on strictness --- need not be minutely detailed but must be
substantial.

**Independent Counsel:** Recommended, not required. Courts examine
whether opportunity existed.

**Notarization:** STRONGLY RECOMMENDED / effectively required. Required
if prenup addresses real estate or estate provisions (which virtually
all do).

**Witnesses:** TWO WITNESSES strongly recommended / required for real
estate and estate provisions. Under § 732.702, waivers of spousal estate
rights require two subscribing witnesses. Best practice: ALWAYS have two
disinterested witnesses + notarization.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. FL attorneys recommend
signing at least 30 days before wedding.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/limited/eliminated. Broad freedom.

**Unconscionability:** At time of execution.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support.

**Unique Rules:** FL\'s UPAA modifications: death provisions must comply
with Probate Code formalities (§ 732.701-702) requiring 2 subscribing
witnesses. Clerical errors can invalidate. Lifestyle clauses generally
not enforceable. Sunset clauses allowed if clearly drafted.

**Key Case Law:** McNamara v. McNamara (2010): Interstate enforceability
confirmed.

**Platform Notes:** Self-service possible. MUST instruct: 2 witnesses +
notary at signing. Even though not technically required for all
provisions, virtually every prenup touches real estate or estate rights.

**Indiana** *[VERIFIED]* **⚠ EXTREME HARDSHIP OVERRIDE | POSTNUPS LIMITED**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution (all property, including premarital, can be divided)

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes. Ind. Code §§ 31-11-3-1 to 31-11-3-10. Titled "Uniform Premarital Agreement Act."

**Statute:** Ind. Code §§ 31-11-3-1 to 31-11-3-10

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both parties. Enforceable without consideration (§ 31-11-3-4).

**Financial Disclosure:** Required. Full disclosure of assets, liabilities, income, and value of holdings. Financial schedules should be attached to the agreement. Failure to disclose can invalidate.

**Independent Counsel:** Not required. Recommended. Courts consider availability of counsel as factor in voluntariness analysis. One source notes mandatory mediation may be required for disputes over voidable provisions.

**Notarization:** Not required. Recommended.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. Presenting agreement close to wedding increases duress risk.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated per § 31-11-3-5(a)(4). ⚠ EXTREME HARDSHIP OVERRIDE (§ 31-11-3-8): If a provision modifying/eliminating spousal support causes one party "extreme hardship under circumstances not reasonably foreseeable at the time of execution," a court may require the other party to provide spousal maintenance "to the extent necessary to avoid extreme hardship." This is BROADER than standard UPAA "public assistance" safety valve — it covers extreme hardship generally, not just eligibility for public assistance programs. This is a key differentiator from most UPAA states.

**Unconscionability:** At time of execution. Agreement not enforceable if party proves (1) involuntary execution OR (2) unconscionable when executed (§ 31-11-3-8). Standard UPAA disclosure defense applies.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support. § 31-11-3-5(b): "A premarital agreement may not adversely affect the right of a child to support."

**Unique Rules:** ⚠ POSTNUPTIAL AGREEMENTS SEVERELY LIMITED — Indiana law says NOTHING about postnuptials, and case law requires couples to be in a state of marital breakdown before executing one. You CANNOT proactively create a postnuptial agreement in a stable marriage. This makes the PRENUP the ONLY opportunity for marital financial planning in Indiana. Indiana is an "all-in" equitable distribution state — without a prenup, ALL property (including premarital) is subject to division, making prenups especially valuable. Amendments must be in writing and signed by both. Infidelity/lifestyle clauses likely unenforceable but no specific case law on point.

**Key Case Law:** Wagner v. Wagner, 888 N.E.2d 924 (Ind. Ct. App.) — enforcement principles. Rider v. Rider — unconscionability comparison test (compare situations of both parties, not just public assistance eligibility).

**Platform Notes:** Good self-service candidate. ⚠ CRITICAL SELLING POINT: Indiana is one of the few states where postnuptials are essentially unavailable for stable marriages — the prenup is the ONLY shot. Use this in state-specific marketing. The "extreme hardship" override (broader than public assistance) means platform should include foreseeability language and hardship provisions.

**Missouri** *[VERIFIED]* **⚠ NO UPAA | NOTARIZATION OR WITNESSES REQ | "FREELY FAIRLY KNOWINGLY" STANDARD**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** NOT adopted. Governed by RSMo § 451.220 + case law.

**Statute:** RSMo § 451.220: "All marriage contracts whereby any estate, real or personal, in this state, is intended to be secured or conveyed to any person or persons, or whereby such estate may be affected in law or equity, shall be in writing, and acknowledged by each of the contracting parties, or proved by one or more subscribing witnesses."

**Spousal Support:** CAN be addressed. Courts examine fairness of alimony provisions. Spousal maintenance governed by Mo. Rev. Stat. § 452.335.

**Unconscionability:** Missouri defines unconscionable as "inequality so strong, gross, and manifest that it must be impossible to state it to a [person] of common sense without producing an exclamation at the inequality of it." **Key Case Law:** In re Marriage of Thomas, 199 S.W.3d 847 (Mo. App. S. Dist. 2006) — upheld prenup signed 2 days before wedding where party had own attorney and full disclosure. Short v. Short, 356 S.W.3d 235 (2011) — declining to hire attorney despite having resources not grounds for challenge. Chapman v. Corbin, 316 S.W.2d 880 (1958) — postnuptial property settlement valid between parties.

**Platform Notes:** ⚠ MUST GENERATE EITHER NOTARY ACKNOWLEDGMENT BLOCKS OR WITNESS SIGNATURE BLOCKS (statutory requirement, not optional). Neuhoff (1978): any provision purporting to limit support of parties while still married is void ab initio (from the beginning).

**New Jersey** *[VERIFIED]* **⚠ 2013 AMENDMENTS STRENGTHENED ENFORCEMENT | CLEAR AND CONVINCING STANDARD | ANNEXED ASSET STATEMENT REQ**

**Property Regime:** Equitable Distribution

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Yes. N.J.S.A. §§ 37:2-31 to 37:2-41. Called "Uniform Premarital and Pre-Civil Union Agreement Act." Original UPAA adopted 1988. ⚠ SIGNIFICANTLY AMENDED June 27, 2013 to dramatically strengthen enforceability. Also amended 2006 to include civil unions.

**Statute:** N.J.S.A. §§ 37:2-31 to 37:2-41

**Writing & Signatures:** Written. Signed by both parties. Enforceable without consideration. ⚠ MUST INCLUDE ANNEXED STATEMENT OF CURRENT ASSETS of both parties (N.J.S.A. § 37:2-33). This is a unique NJ requirement — the asset statement must be physically attached to the agreement.

Written waiver of disclosure permitted but must be knowing and voluntary and explicitly stated.

The current version is less specific but independent counsel still strengthens enforceability.

**Notarization:** Not required. Recommended.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. Courts skeptical of prenups signed within days of presentation. 1-2 weeks minimum between disclosure and execution recommended.

The 2013 amendments DELETED the old provision that tested unconscionability at enforcement time — now only tested at signing.

**Unconscionability:** ⚠ POST-2013: Tested at TIME OF SIGNING ONLY — the 2013 amendments deleted the provision allowing courts to consider unconscionability at time of enforcement. This is a major strengthening of enforceability. Changed circumstances during the marriage are NO LONGER a basis for invalidation. ⚠ CLEAR AND CONVINCING EVIDENCE standard — the burden on the party challenging the prenup is higher than normal "preponderance of evidence." Must prove by clear and convincing evidence that: (a) involuntary execution, OR (b) unconscionable per the NJ definition.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child custody or support. § 37:2-35.

**Unique Rules:** ⚠ THREE DIFFERENT LEGAL FRAMEWORKS depending on when prenup was signed: (1) Before Nov 2, 1988: governed by case law. (2) Nov 2, 1988 to June 27, 2013: governed by original UPAA version (unconscionability tested at enforcement time). (3) After June 27, 2013: governed by current strengthened version (unconscionability at signing only, clear and convincing standard). Courts have "consistently found that prenuptial agreements are valid and enforceable and should be welcomed and encouraged." Mid-marriage (postnuptial) agreements viewed with "great skepticism" — courts see them as "inherently coercive" because one party's desire to preserve the marriage creates unequal footing.

**Key Case Law:** Standard UPAA enforcement under the statutory framework. In re Evans, 227 N.J. — burden of proof standards. Multiple cases interpreting the 1988-2013 version (most current divorces involve agreements from this era).

**Platform Notes:** ⚠ For post-June 2013 agreements, NJ is one of the MOST enforcement-friendly states — unconscionability at signing only + clear and convincing standard. **Wisconsin** *[VERIFIED]* **⚠ COMMUNITY PROPERTY (UMPA) | "MARITAL PROPERTY AGREEMENTS" | STATUTORY FORM OPTION | COMPLEX**

**Property Regime:** Community Property under the Uniform Marital Property Act (UMPA/WMPA) — NOT the same framework as CA/TX/AZ community property. Wisconsin is the ONLY state to adopt the UMPA.

**UPAA/UPMAA:** Neither. Wisconsin uses its OWN framework under the Wisconsin Marital Property Act (WMPA). Prenups are called "marital property agreements" and governed by Wis. Stat. § 766.58. Wisconsin also has statutory agreement forms (§§ 766.587, 766.588, 766.589).

**Statute:** Wis. Stat. § 766.58 (marital property agreements — main statute); § 766.585 (agreements before determination date); §§ 766.587-766.589 (statutory forms)

**Writing & Signatures:** Written ("document"). Signed by both parties. Only the spouses may be parties. Enforceable without consideration.

**Financial Disclosure:** Required for custom agreements under § 766.58(6): Fair and reasonable disclosure under the circumstances + notice of other spouse's property/obligations. ⚠ HOWEVER: Statutory form agreements under § 766.587 are enforceable WITHOUT disclosure — this is unique. The statutory form classifies all property as individual property of the owner.

**Independent Counsel:** Not required. § 766.58(8) explicitly states: "the fact that both parties are represented by one counsel or that one party is represented by counsel and the other party is not represented by counsel does not by itself make a marital property agreement unconscionable or otherwise affect its enforceability." This is unusually permissive.

**Notarization:** Not required. Recommended.

**Witnesses:** Not required.

**Waiting/Timing:** No statutory waiting period. "Determination date" concept is unique to Wisconsin — relates to when WMPA provisions took effect for a particular couple.

**Spousal Support:** Can be modified/eliminated BUT with TWO restrictions: (1) § 766.58(9)(a): Modification/elimination during marriage cannot result in spouse having less than "necessary and adequate support, taking into consideration all sources of support." (2) § 766.58(9)(b): Public assistance safety valve at time of dissolution or death — court can require other spouse or estate to provide support.

**Unconscionability:** At time agreement was MADE. § 766.58(6): Agreement not enforceable if party proves: (a) unconscionable when made, OR (b) involuntary execution, OR (c) no fair/reasonable disclosure AND no notice of other spouse's property/obligations. Note: (c) uses "AND" between its two sub-requirements — party must show BOTH lack of disclosure AND lack of notice. This is slightly different from standard UPAA which has three sub-requirements.

**Child Provisions:** Cannot predetermine child support. § 766.58(2): "A marital property agreement may not adversely affect the right of a child to support."

**Unique Rules:** MOST COMPLEX prenup framework in the country. Key unique features: (1) Called "MARITAL PROPERTY AGREEMENTS" — not prenuptial agreements. (2) STATUTORY FORM OPTION (§ 766.587) — Wisconsin offers a statutory form that classifies all property as individual property of the owner, enforceable WITHOUT financial disclosure. However, this form does NOT affect: spousal support duties, property division at divorce (§ 767.61), maintenance payments (§ 767.56), or surviving spouse's elective share (§ 861.02). So the statutory form is limited in scope. (3) "DETERMINATION DATE" — unique Wisconsin concept that determines when WMPA provisions apply to a particular couple. (4) BINDING ARBITRATION permitted — spouses can agree to submit controversies to binding arbitration. (5) Community property under UMPA is DIFFERENT from CA/TX framework — uses concepts like "marital property," "individual property," "deferred marital property." (6) One attorney for both parties explicitly does NOT make agreement unconscionable. (7) Grandfathering provisions for pre-WMPA agreements (§ 766.58(12)). (8) Digital property addressed (§ 766.58(7)(c)). (9) Deferred employment benefits and IRAs have special classification rules.

**Key Case Law:** Button v. Button — landmark WI prenup case. Garczynski, "A Decade Post-Button v. Button: Drafting Prenuptial Agreements," Wis. Law. (1999).

**Platform Notes:** ⚠ MOST COMPLEX STATE for platform implementation. Must use "marital property agreement" terminology (not "prenuptial agreement"). Platform needs to understand UMPA community property framework (different from CA/TX). Consider offering the STATUTORY FORM OPTION (§ 766.587) as a simpler alternative for couples who just want individual property classification — but with clear warnings about its limitations (doesn't affect divorce property division, maintenance, or elective share). Custom agreements under § 766.58 require full treatment. Include arbitration clause option. Community property clauses must use UMPA-specific terminology. Self-service possible but requires significant Wisconsin-specific customization.
